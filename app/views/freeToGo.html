Visualizing Headring

A wearable product that allows the visually impaired move freely.

Project Details

Background

The idea arose in 2014 HackShanghai, a college hackathon held by students from NYU Shanghai. In the 24 epic hours of development, we built a wearable auxiliary product that helps the blind to avoid obstacles and navigate to a given destination. Our team got in the general top 10 and won the Audience Favorite Prize out of over 80 teams. We continued to improve the product in the following months.

Problem Space

â€œ There are 5 million people estimated to be blind who are of productive age and need independent mobility to work or go to school and be part of the mainstream. Prof. Meenakshi Balakrishnan, Indian Institute of Technology

As it is been said above, there is a large number of people who are blind and need to be independent and safe enough while moving. To be safe when going outside, two purposes for blind persons have to be reached:

  • to effectively avoid the possibly dangerous obstacles;
  • to navigate to the destination by themselves (e.g. home, school, company, etc)

Thus, we wanted to help the visually impaired to move without encountering obstacles, and navigate to a specific destination on their own. We have observed many scenarios in which this group of people use old-school guiding tools for going out. Those tools cannot solve the both problems simultaneously.

  • The tools help blind persons sense limited distance from obstacles and the range of things. Standard canes, for instance, are poor at detecting obstacles that are above waist height and do not have a touch-point on the ground, such as tree branches sticking out into the path. Besides, they could just sense the things within around one meter. The distance is sometimes too close for people to instantly avoid the obstacles.
  • The blind are not always led to the safe space. Although those guiding tools may help people avoid obstacles, they do not know where is safe and where is not. For example, people might mistakenly think they are walking along with sidewalks when they go across the roads full of cars. Those tools cannot tell the difference.
  • The tools cannot navigate. For the blind people, to find out the destinations is challenging. Guide dogs might help sometimes, but just for the places the dogs are familiar with. Getting to the places they have never been to would be extremely difficult.

They Need More Safety While Moving

Get more information on what they face in front of them
To address the problems above, the blind-guiding tools should be put into more intelligence. We have conducted a literature review on methods of object recognition and found that there are two major ways to detect objects: ultrasound technology and image recognition. Intensive researchs have been working on improving recognition accuracy of image recognition algorithms. Plus, using ultrasound technology to effectively recognize objects could be costly since it needs several additional devices. Therefore, we chose image recognition as our main technique for obstacle detection. We utilized the principle of Binocular Stereo Vision, which could help our algorithms to reconstruct 3D pictures of the obejects and result in the positions and distances.

Know which direction they should move to in next step
We designed a hardware system, a headring built in two cameras and nine vibration modules towards different directions. Cameras collect and process real-time image flow through the algorithms. When approaching obstacles are detected, corresponding vibration module will vibrate to inform the user to avoid. For example, if there is a tree standing on the left, vibration module towards the left will vibrate so that users could know they should avoid and move to relatively right in next step.

Moreover, we developed a mobile app combined with SDK from Google Maps and Baidu Maps. Through Bluetooth headset, the blind can use voice commands to trigger voice navigation to the destination they set and then be guided within the route.

Conceptual Design for the Product

Be connected with close relatives and friends
Our mobile app allows user's relatives or friends to connect with the user. The app constantly keeps track of user's real-time location so that the relatives and friends could be sure user is safe at the moment. When user walks off the supposed route for a specific amount of time, a deviation alert will be sent to relatives and friends.

User and relatives or friends can have a chat if necessary. If relatives use text message, text-to-speech module powered by iFLYTEK will translate the message into speech, which the blind users can hear. They are also able to use the app to request the cameras to send back the pictures of what the user faces at the time.

As it is proposed above, the whole process involves close interaction among people, hardware and software. Each unit is designed to guarantee the safety of blind users.

Personal Thoughts

As we tackled the problems for those who are blind, I felt very deep empathy for the disabled. We cannot imagine how inconvenient for the visually impaired to commute to work or school with the traditional canes or guide dogs unless we immerse ourselves into their situations. Through this project I believe that with the help of rapidly enhancing technologies, the blind would be capable of moving more freely and safely. We hope this project would be a great beginning of the vision.
